## PART 1 — WHAT IS TCS NQT AND WHY IT MATTERS

TCS NQT (National Qualifier Test) is TCS's single-exam hiring gateway for freshers. One test, one score, three possible outcomes — Ninja, Digital, or Prime. The score is valid for 2 years and is accepted by multiple other corporates beyond TCS.

Batches eligible: 2024, 2025, 2026 (B.Tech / B.E / M.Tech / M.E / MCA / M.Sc)
Academic requirement: Minimum 60% or 6.0 CGPA across 10th, 12th, Graduation. No active backlogs.
Register at: nextstep.tcs.com

### ACTUAL PREVIOUS YEAR NUMERICAL QUESTIONS (student-reported)

Q1. A man has to travel 50 km in 2 hours. He covered 20 km in the first hour then stopped for 10 minutes for refuelling. By what factor should he increase his speed to complete the journey on time?
Answer: 1.8
(Time left = 50 min. Distance left = 30 km. New speed needed = 36 km/h. Original = 20 km/h. Factor = 36/20 = 1.8)

Q2. The diameter of a pizza is 30 cm. What is the area of the upper surface of a sector whose arc length is 8 cm?
Answer: 60 cm²
(Area = (1/2) × r × l = (1/2) × 15 × 8 = 60)

Q3. The average of 10 numbers is 40. One number was wrongly entered as 40 instead of 45. What is the correct average?
Answer: 40.5
(Correct sum = 400 - 40 + 45 = 405. Average = 405/10 = 40.5)

Q4. A man covers three equal distances at speeds of 10, 20, and 30 km/h. Find the average speed for the whole journey.
Answer: ~16.36 km/h
(Harmonic mean of 3 equal distances: 3 / (1/10 + 1/20 + 1/30) = 3 / (6/60 + 3/60 + 2/60) = 3 / (11/60) = 180/11 ≈ 16.36)

Q5. The average monthly income of P and Q is ₹5050. Q and R is ₹6250. P and R is ₹5200. Find monthly income of P.
Answer: ₹4000
(P + Q = 10100, Q + R = 12500, P + R = 10400. Adding all: 2(P+Q+R) = 33000, so P+Q+R = 16500. R = 16500 - 10100 = 6400. P = 16500 - 12500 = 4000)

Q6. A pupil's marks were wrongly entered as 83 instead of 63. Due to this the average for the class increased by 1/2. Find the number of pupils.
Answer: 40
(Difference = 20. 20/n = 0.5. n = 40)

Q7. A right triangle with sides 3, 4, 5 cm is rotated about the side of 3 cm to form a cone. Find the volume.
Answer: 16π cm³
(r = 4, h = 3. Volume = (1/3)π r²h = (1/3)π(16)(3) = 16π)

Q8. Two balls are selected and the probability of getting one red and one blue is 1/6. Find the number of red balls.
Answer: 4 (when x = 2, total red = x + 2 = 4)

Q9. Two spheres — the difference between their surface areas is 1408π cm². Find diameter of bigger sphere.
(Surface area difference = 4π(R² - r²) = 1408π. Solve with given ratio or second value)

### 5B — VERBAL ABILITY (25 questions, 25 min)

This is the easiest section to score if you're consistent. Focus on question types, not raw vocabulary cramming.

**Topics and approximate weightage:**
- Reading Comprehension (RC) — 2 passages, 5–6 questions each. Passages are always new but question types repeat.
- Error Spotting / Sentence Correction — grammar rules, subject-verb agreement, tense consistency
- Fill in the Blanks — contextual vocabulary
- Para Jumbles — sentence reordering
- Synonyms and Antonyms — moderate vocab level
- Sentence Completion

**Grammar rules that repeat constantly:**
- Collective nouns (committee, team, jury) take singular verbs
- "Either...or" and "neither...nor" — verb agrees with the noun closest to it
- "Each", "every", "anyone", "everyone" take singular verbs
- Misplaced modifiers — the modifier must be adjacent to what it modifies
- Subjunctive mood — "If I were..." not "If I was..."

**RC strategy:** Read the last sentence of each paragraph first to get the conclusion. Then skim. Answer inference questions last.

Verbal PYQs are the least reusable section because RC passages are always new. Focus on question type patterns, not actual previous passages.

### 5C — REASONING ABILITY (20 questions, 25 min)

Highest variance section. High scorers here separate Ninja from Digital.

**High priority topics (carry maximum weight):**
- Logical Deduction / Syllogisms
- Letter Series and Number Series (pattern identification)
- Seating Arrangements (linear and circular)
- Blood Relations
- Puzzles (box stacking, floor arrangements)
- Data Sufficiency

**Medium priority:**
- Direction and Distance
- Coding-Decoding
- Clocks and Calendars

**Low priority:**
- Venn Diagrams
- Decision Making
- Attention to Detail

The 2026 format: ~30 questions, 50 minutes across Foundation and Advanced reasoning combined. Puzzles carry the highest single-topic weight.

**Typical reasoning question types seen in previous cycles:**

Syllogism example (actual pattern):
Premises: All cats are dogs. Some dogs are fish.
Conclusion I: Some cats are fish. — Cannot be determined.
Conclusion II: Some fish are dogs. — True.

Series example (actual pattern):
2, 6, 12, 20, 30, ?
Answer: 42 (differences are 4, 6, 8, 10, 12 — second differences constant at 2)

Blood Relation example (actual 2025 question):
"Karan is Ritu's son, making him Akshita's brother. His wife would therefore be Akshita's ___."
Answer: Sister-in-law

Floor/Box arrangement (actual 2025 pattern):
Eight boxes P, Q, R, S, T, U, V, W are kept one above the other.
[Conditions given] → Find position of specific box.
Strategy: Make a column diagram and fill constraints top-to-bottom.

Compound Interest series (actual PYQ):
If a principal doubles in 5 years at CI, find the rate.
Answer: ~14.87% ≈ 15%
(2 = (1 + r)^5. r = 2^(1/5) - 1 ≈ 0.1487)

### 5D — PROGRAMMING LOGIC (MCQ section, part of Foundation)

This section is separate from actual coding. It tests flowcharts, pseudocode tracing, and output prediction. Being good at coding doesn't guarantee a high score here — the framing is different.

**Topics:**
- Data types (C-based)
- Input/output operations
- Variables, registers, and scope
- Functions and recursion (output tracing)
- Control structures (if-else, loops — what is the output?)
- Pointers (basic)
- Arrays (index, traversal output)
- OOP concepts (for Java/C++ questions)

Seven patterns that appear in ~70% of NQT Foundation sessions (2023–2025):

1. Loop with counter — "What is the output of this for loop?"
2. Recursive function — "What does f(5) return?"
3. Pointer arithmetic — "What does *p + 1 print?"
4. Array index output — "What is arr[3] after this operation?"
5. Scope of variable — "Which line causes an error?"
6. Swap without temp — XOR swap output prediction
7. String traversal — "What does this while loop print for 'HELLO'?"

If you hand-trace these 7 patterns twice with actual code, you will recognize them in the exam within 20 seconds.

### 5E — ADVANCED CODING (2–3 problems, 90 minutes)

This is where Digital and Prime bands are decided. One complete solution gets you Digital territory. Two complete solutions gets you Prime territory.

Difficulty: Easy-Medium (Q1) + Medium-Hard (Q2) + Hard (Q3, if present)

Allowed languages: C, C++, Java, Python, Perl

**Topic distribution from previous cycles:**
- Array manipulation — most common
- String processing — second most common
- Number theory (palindrome, perfect number, prime check)
- Pattern printing
- Sorting variations
- Linked list (rare but appears)
- Greedy (appears in Advanced Q2/Q3)
- Dynamic Programming (appears rarely, mostly recognizable variants)

Arrays + Strings + Greedy beats trying to learn DP from scratch. Prioritize in this order.

**Q1. Exercise Duration (Easy)**
A user tracks workouts. Given start time (HH:MM) and end time (HH:MM), calculate total duration in minutes.
Input: 09:30, 10:45
Output: 75

```python
def exercise_duration(start, end):
    sh, sm = map(int, start.split(':'))
    eh, em = map(int, end.split(':'))
    return (eh * 60 + em) - (sh * 60 + sm)
```

**Q2. Palindrome Check (Easy-Medium)**
Given a number N, check if it's a palindrome.
Input: 121 → Output: True
Input: 123 → Output: False

```python
def is_palindrome(n):
    s = str(n)
    return s == s[::-1]
```

**Q3. Perfect Number Check (Medium)**
A perfect number equals the sum of its proper divisors. Check if N is perfect.
Input: 6 → Output: True (1+2+3=6)
Input: 12 → Output: False

```python
def is_perfect(n):
    if n <= 1:
        return False
    total = sum(i for i in range(1, n) if n % i == 0)
    return total == n
```

**Q4. Word Frequency Counter (Medium)**
Count the frequency of each word in a given string.
Input: "hello world hello"
Output: hello: 2, world: 1

```python
def word_freq(sentence):
    from collections import Counter
    return Counter(sentence.split())
```

**Q5. Pairs with Specific Difference (Medium)**
Given an array and a number K, count all pairs where |a - b| = K.
Input: arr = [1, 5, 3, 4, 2], K = 2 → Output: 3 (pairs: (1,3), (3,5), (2,4))

```python
def count_pairs(arr, k):
    count = 0
    s = set(arr)
    for num in s:
        if num + k in s:
            count += 1
    return count
```

**Q6. Move Zeros to End (Easy, actual PrepInsta verified)**
Given array of N integers, push all 0s to the end while maintaining order of non-zero elements.
Input: [4, 5, 0, 1, 9, 0, 5, 0]
Output: [4, 5, 1, 9, 5, 0, 0, 0]

```python
def move_zeros(arr):
    result = [x for x in arr if x != 0]
    result += [0] * (len(arr) - len(result))
    return result
```

**Q7. Odd Occurring Element (Advanced)**
In an array where every element appears an even number of times except one, find the odd-occurring element. Pairs must appear together.
Input: 5 elements → 2 2 3 1 1
Output: 3
(XOR-based approach if allowed to be O(n), or binary search for O(log n))

```python
def odd_occurring(arr):
    result = 0
    for x in arr:
        result ^= x
    return result
```

**Q8. Subset Sum Count (Advanced)**
Count all subsets of a given array whose sum equals a given target.
Input: arr = [1, 2, 3], target = 3
Output: 2 (subsets: {1,2} and {3})

```python
def count_subsets(arr, target):
    count = 0
    n = len(arr)
    for i in range(1 << n):
        subset_sum = sum(arr[j] for j in range(n) if i & (1 << j))
        if subset_sum == target:
            count += 1
    return count
```

**Q9. Jump Game (Medium, appeared in 2025 shift)**
Given array where each element is max jump length from that position, check if you can reach the last index from index 0.
Input: [2, 3, 1, 1, 4] → Output: True
Input: [3, 2, 1, 0, 4] → Output: False

```python
def can_jump(nums):
    max_reach = 0
    for i, n in enumerate(nums):
        if i > max_reach:
            return False
        max_reach = max(max_reach, i + n)
    return True
```

**Q10. Nth Term in Mixed Series (Classic TCS, appears repeatedly)**
Two series are interleaved to form a single series. Find the Nth term.
Example: Series A: 2, 4, 6, 8... Series B: 3, 9, 27, 81...
Mixed: 2, 3, 4, 9, 6, 27, 8, 81...
If N is odd → N//2+1 th term of A = 2*(N//2+1)
If N is even → N//2 th term of B = 3^(N//2)

```python
def nth_mixed(n):
    if n % 2 == 1:
        pos = (n + 1) // 2
        return 2 * pos
    else:
        pos = n // 2
        return 3 ** pos
```

## PART 7 — WHAT TO EXPECT IN 2026 (PREDICTED TOPICS)

Based on patterns across 2022–2025 cycles and the 2026 syllabus update:

Numerical: Same topic distribution. Percentages, TSD, and Number System will dominate. Expect at least 2 geometry questions (cylinder, cone, sphere surface area/volume).

Verbal: RC passages will be new but question types identical. Expect 2 passages. Grammar focus: subject-verb agreement, conditionals, para-jumbles with 5-part sentences.

Reasoning: Expect 1 large puzzle (8-element box/floor arrangement), at least 2 series questions, and 2–3 syllogism questions. Data sufficiency is returning.

Programming Logic: Flowchart tracing and pseudocode output is increasing. Expect at least 3 "what is the output" questions with loops or recursion.

Advanced Coding:
- Q1: Array manipulation (move zeros, find duplicate, rotate array) — Easy
- Q2: String processing (anagram check, palindrome variants, word reversal) — Medium
- Q3 (if present): Greedy or DP variant (jump game, coin change, subset sum) — Hard

Key 2026 change: Verbal and Programming Logic now have independent sectional floors. You cannot bomb one section and compensate with another. Both must be cleared.

## PART 10 — EXAM DAY CHECKLIST

Before going in:
- Valid photo ID (Aadhaar, college ID)
- Print of admit card
- Reach 30 minutes early
- No phone inside exam hall

During the exam:
- Read the 2-minute instruction screen carefully — do not skip
- You cannot go back to a previous question once you click Next
- Guess every single unanswered question — no negative marking
- Solve easy questions first within each section's fixed order
- For coding: solve Q1 fully before touching Q2. Partial score exists for test cases.
- If the compiler gives errors, check for extra spaces or wrong input format — this is a known TCS iON platform issue
- Do not switch tabs even for a second

Section time allocation suggestion:
- Numerical: avg 70 sec per question, save last 2 min for review
- Verbal: avg 55 sec per question, RC questions last
- Reasoning: avg 70 sec per question, skip and mark if stuck on a puzzle
- Advanced Aptitude: avg 100 sec per question — harder than Foundation
- Coding: 35–40 min on Q1 to get full marks, then Q2 with remaining time
